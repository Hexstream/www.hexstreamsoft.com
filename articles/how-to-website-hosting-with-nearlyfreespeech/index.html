How-to: Website hosting with NearlyFreeSpeech

Easily managing a low-tech, dirt cheap static website with git, using linux.

INCOMPLETE. IN PROGRESS.

I'm not affiliated with NearlyFreeSpeech, just one of their happy customers and fans.

This article is intended for a broader audience than most of the content on this website.

This article assumes you're using linux and have at least basic command line skills.

⚓ NFS hosting » Preamble » My website history

I've been using NearlyFreeSpeech to host this website (hexstreamsoft.com) since 2011.

At first, I didn't have much content, so I was content with updating the site through fairly manual means, sometimes even just sshing in, launching a remote emacs instance and making minor edits that way.

Obviously that was a really bad way to go about things, but fortunately in February 2013 I somehow figured out that NearlyFreeSpeech does have support for updating one's website via git pushes. This has been a revolution for me.

⚓ NFS hosting » Preamble » My motivations for writing this article

For some reason, NearlyFreeSpeech does not advertise their git support anywhere. I think that's a shame, since in my view it's one of the best ways to go about things. Hence, I've decided to write this guide to describe my setup, which I love so much and might appeal to others too.

(I dream of even better setups, but that's a discussion for another day.)

I'd also like to say that managing your own website is a lot of fun! I think there's too many people that settle for, say, a hosted blog (such as tumblr, wordpress or blogger) just because they overestimate the complexities of managing a "real" website. It really isn't that hard. I hope this article helps some people getting started by giving some good answers to important questions that might otherwise discourage them.

Warning: Opinionated!

Low-tech, static website ⚓

Forget complicated dynamic frameworks, for simpler needs it's often better to stick to the fundamentals like raw HTML(5) and CSS(3). If you want to upgrade to a more complex but flexible solution later, most of what you've learned will still be helpful. You'll be able to reuse your knowledge.

Raw HTML(5) and CSS(3) also has the advantage of being a more conservative choice. Pretty much every web framework out there says they're the best, but by definition they can't all be. If you start with the fundamentals, you'll be better prepared to evaluate their claims and make your own decisions, or maybe even write your own framework, if you're so inclined.

Dirt cheap, but not "free" either. No bullshit. ⚓

If you have relatively low traffic levels, even the cheapest for-pay services would traditionally give you tons of bandwidth (and other resources) that you don't need and make you pay for it all, regardless of the portion that you actually use.

NearlyFreeSpeech's innovative pay-as-you-go pricing really saves the situation here. And they've been doing it for more than a decade already (since 2002), so that's proof that the model works and is sustainable.

Hosting this website (hexstreamsoft.com) on NearlyFreeSpeech has cost me
a mere 2.30$ in total for all of 2013. I'm literally laughing on my way to the bank!

Now, you might be wondering: there are free hosting services out there, wouldn't 0$ per year be even better? NOT! There's a saying that's making the rounds lately that I generally agree with: if you're not the customer, then you're the product. In other words, if a company is giving you services for free, then they'll have to recoup their costs by making money with you in other ways than directly charging you, and more often than not those other ways will be harmful to you in some way. For instance, some free hosts insert ads in your pages, for their own profit. I don't know for you, but to me that's unacceptable. I don't want random ads I don't control to distract my users from my content.

➔ Now, you need to create a new site ("step 5"). Login as necessary.
(In fact, due to some security checks it's better if you're not already logged in, else you'd need to follow the equivalent link on NearlyFreeSpeech...)

You'll be presented with a nice "wizard" that streamlines the initial setup.
We'll do these steps together, since I have some extra explaining to do.

Note that all the decisions you take with this wizard can be changed later by going into your account and updating the appropriate settings, but this is outside the scope of this how-to.

Unfortunately, due to the way the wizard is coded, once you have completed a step it's not possible to go back without losing your state. In that case, the easiest thing to do is to logout (using the link at the top right of the page) and start over.)

⚓ NFS hosting » Initial setup » NFS site setup » ② "Use another name?"

➔ If you want your new site to be accessible via a custom domain name, enter it to set it up as an alias.

Else, your site will only be accessible through the "short name", as short-site-name.nfshost.net.

If you want to use your example.com domain name, then I recommend you enter www.example.com as the alias and later setup a redirect from example.com to www.example.com.

It's also possible to do it the other way around, but I like to do it this way because a web server is just one of many different services you could run using a domain name, so to me it doesn't make much sense to give it priority over services.

⚓ NFS hosting » Initial setup » NFS site setup » ②½ DNS and email forwarding

If you did elect to use a custom domain name, the wizard will now show an intermediary step to ask if you want to manage DNS and email through NearlyFreeSpeech or if you want to manage it yourself (or through another third-party service).

Now, you could use NearlyFreeSpeech to manage your DNS and email forwarding, if you want, but they do charge a certain amount for it. Most registrars (through which all domain registration is done) already provide such services for free, so I'll assume you'll want to use your registrar's services. In that case, you'll want to check the I will set up my own DNS and email option.

⚓ NFS hosting » Initial setup » NFS site setup » ③ "Server Type"

➔ Pick the [Production] Static Content - No PHP or CGI (Stochastic) option.

This is the only option that has Base Daily Cost: none.

The other options are outside the scope of this article.

⚓ NFS hosting » Initial setup » NFS site setup » ④ "Confirm Site Details"

➔ Simply review the information and click on Create Now if everything's fine, else there's a handy button to start over.
